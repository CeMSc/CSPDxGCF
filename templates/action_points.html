Conflict Assessment

Considering the (sometimes complex) interactions between the impacts of climate change and conflict is particularly important in an area vulnerable to climate change and affected by underlying tensions, whether they are rooted in ethnic, economic, linguistic or social differences. The impacts of climate change can worsen these dividing lines, risking the smooth implementation of project activities. In addition, projects can sometimes unintentionally make the situation worse if the project is ‘blind’ to those conflict drivers. But equally, being aware of the conflict drivers enables the project to have a ‘peace-positive’ impact.

A conflict assessment (or a climate-conflict assessment) is an important first step to understand the underlying drivers of conflict in your project area, to anticipate problems, to ensure your project is not making them worse, and to develop ‘peace-positive’ solutions. Using a conflict ‘lens’ is an important way to think through the risks of the project being derailed by, or adding to, conflict. It can be done as a part of the broad socioeconomic risk assessment, or as a separate, additional exercise.

The process of developing your conflict assessment can be as important as the product itself. It is an opportunity to engage with your stakeholders to better understand their needs and concerns. It is also a chance to involve as many of the project team as possible to raise their awareness of the political and social dynamics in the project area. The conflict assessment should be a ‘living document’ – something that you can come back to and use time and again as the project evolves.

The conflict assessment should contain the following elements, depending on the needs of your specific project. First, you can use the assessment already done under the climate rationale on the current and projected climate impacts to assess the coping and adaptation capacity and how the project may affect conflict dynamics in the project area. Second, you can build on the mapping of the social and environmental dynamics around the natural resources affected by the project (e.g. land, water, energy) to forecast how the project might/ will change access, control and use of those resources. Finally, it will be key to assess the stakeholders who will be affected by the project, how and to what extent.

The conflict assessment needs to:

  • Involve local stakeholders: This is essential to ensuring that the project properly anticipates potential conflict issues that could derail the project, ensures that the project does no harm by exacerbating underlying causes of tension, and maximises the opportunities to support peace in the project area.

  • Consider the potential intersections between climate change impacts and conflict drivers: Considering the (sometimes complex) interactions between the impacts of climate change and conflict is particularly important in an area vulnerable to climate change and affected by underlying tensions, whether they are rooted in ethnic, economic, linguistic or social differences. The impacts of climate change can worsen these dividing lines, and risk the smooth implementation of project activities. In addition, projects can sometimes unintentionally make the situation worse if the project is ‘blind’ to those conflict drivers. Equally, being aware of the conflict drivers enables the project to have a ‘peace-positive’ impact.

  • Consider the potential constraining factors for the participation, benefit and empowerment of specific groups: Considering how stakeholders can participate in, benefit from and be empowered by the project is a critical part of designing a successful, impactful project. This is particularly important for sometimes marginalised groups that may otherwise be left out of the design or implementation of the project: such as women, youth, older people, minorities, and the disabled.  Involving these stakeholders in the production of a conflict assessment (through focus group discussions, consultations, and - where appropriate - in the analysis team itself)  is essential to ensuring that the project includes mechanisms to ensure the full participation of these groups in the project and anticipates potential conflict that could derail the project, ensures that the project does no harm by exacerbating underlying causes of tension, and maximises the opportunities to support peace in the project area.

  • Quantify the conflict and security risks in the project: Your conflict assessment should try to outline the conflict and security risks in the proposed project and understand the extent to which they pose threats to the stakeholders of the project, to project staff and/or the successful achievement of the project’s goals. Where appropriate, your funding proposal should identify external conflict as a potential risk and outline a plan for mitigating conflict and promoting peace in the targeted region/area. While developing the Theory of Change of the funding proposal, you can identify the pitfalls and levers to mitigate security impacts and maximise co-benefits to increase the resilience of the most vulnerable people and communities by defining the components that can deepen the rift between communities, or, on the contrary, bring them together.

Mechanisms to minimize potential operational risks caused by insecurity

If your conflict assessment has identified external conflict as a potential threat to the smooth implementation of the project or to the safety of stakeholders and staff, you should consider what kind of mechanisms you can put in place to minimise disruptions to the project.

There are many such measures including:

  • Lines of communication with local authorities and security forces: In medium and high-risk contexts it is particularly important to establish lines of communication with local authorities and security forces over potential security-related threats to the project, beneficiaries, partners or staff (e.g. kidnapping, extortion etc) before they happen. If necessary, list potential mechanisms for engagement with armed groups, in the implementation arrangement, stakeholder engagement plan as well as one of the mitigation measures in the Risk Management and mitigation measures section of the funding proposal.

  • Staff safety: The safety of staff, partners and beneficiaries is a primary concern. While drafting the conflict assessment, you should conduct a gender and social inclusion analysis in line with the objectives of the IFF or climate fund (see for example the GCF Gender Policy) on how the conflict situation is specifically affecting women, girls, men, boys, other genders and gender identities. You should also ensure that the needs of female staff, partners and beneficiaries are fully reflected in staff training in conflict management, contingency plans and monitoring mechanisms.

  • Monitoring systems for operational risks. During and after activities, it is key to monitor both operational risks, as well as the project’s impacts on the security context. Monitoring should not hinder action but rather it should serve as a management tool to correct the course of action of the programme as and when needed. Pre-determined measures/ protocols for such monitoring help to ensure that sufficient attention is paid to ongoing ‘horizon scanning’. Data on the evolution and emergence of sources of tension and cohesion, changing dynamics that can impact the project, and the good implementation of conflict sensitivity measures is particularly relevant. During activities’ rollout, operational risks can be mitigated by robust early-warning systems for insecurity, engagement with arms carriers, and contingency funding for security-related delays.

  • Contingency plans:  Implementing projects in areas of moderate to high risk can be highly unpredictable, prone to delay and disruption. Contingency planning is an important way of ensuring that the project has the means to address sudden changes in the context of the project with as few impacts on results, staff and beneficiaries as possible. It requires assessing the identified risks to the project (such as political instability etc), their likelihood and their impact on project delivery if they came to pass. It then requires you to think through the contingency steps (or mitigating measures) you would take if those risks happened, and ensure that everyone associated is familiar and comfortable with those plans. Contingency steps can take multiple forms depending on the particular context you encounter: flexible delivery timelines, pre-arranged meeting points for staff, a specific budget line for unexpected expenses and so on.

  • Staff training in conflict management: Successfully implementing large-scale climate action projects in medium- and high-risk environments requires a range of specific skills for staff and/ or partners. If staff do not already possess these skills, it is important to invest in building, or refreshing them. These range from staff safety (such as Hostile Environment Awareness Training) to conflict analysis, risk assessment, mediation and conflict management.

  • Crisis response teams/ focal points: While drafting the funding proposal, it is helpful to identify a staff focal point for the conflict and peace dimensions of the project. It may also be useful to identify a crisis response team, or a mix of staff with specific expertise to be able to respond in the event of serious threats to project implementation or staff, partners and/or beneficiaries.

Local power dynamics

Anticipating how the project could interact with prevailing conflict dynamics in the project area is critical to ensuring that the project is, at the very least, conflict sensitive (i.e. it does no harm) and that it can actively support peace and stability. This starts with the conflict assessment and requires an open, enquiring process that includes relevant staff, partners, beneficiaries and stakeholders to systematically gather insights on the project and its potential impact on peace and conflict.

Careful planning helps to mitigate the risks of the project activities themselves contributing to existing problems. Negative impacts can be prevented by regularly engaging stakeholders and addressing their concerns, including through adequate grievance and compensation mechanisms where necessary. Co-benefits can be further leveraged by activities through regular and open communication, which can bring stakeholders closer, reveal opportunities for peace and raise awareness for durable dispute resolution mechanisms.

If the project has a direct impact on natural resources on the ground that are critical to the livelihoods of people living in the project area, then there is a chance that the project could inadvertently create ‘winners’ and ‘losers’ and drive tensions in the project area. It is important to consider these in the project planning stages and throughout the implementation of the project.

Project implementation risks

Projects that fail to take account of underlying conflict dynamics can sometimes make the situation worse. This can happen in a number of ways, such as by ignoring or marginalising one segment of the population or by delivering project interventions that are seen to benefit one group more than another.  It is important to put in place measures to address and mitigate any issues with project delivery as they arise. These could include:

  • A plan to manage stakeholder expectations: Stakeholder engagement is a key component project preparation. For example, the Revised Environmental and Social Policy of the Green Climate Fund (GCF) applies to all activities financed by the GCF, and to both private and public sector accredited entities. In the case of the GCF, requiring accredited entities (AEs) to establish meaningful consultation and engagement processes is a strategic priority embedded in the environmental and social management system for GCF and the environmental and social safeguards, Revised Environmental and Social Policy, Updated Gender Policy and Indigenous Peoples Policy. While drafting the stakeholder engagement plan, you should design strategies to engage in active and open consultation with all stakeholders throughout the lifecycle of the project.

  • Mechanisms of redress and conflict resolution: Credible mechanisms of mediation, conflict resolution and redress that are accessible to stakeholders are an important way to address complaints about the project before they rise to potentially explosive tensions. For example, the GCF mandates the Independent Redress Mechanism which provides recourse to those affected by GCF programmes and projects. Projects can also create ad-hoc mechanisms for redress and conflict resolution as needed by the project, and involving local communities can ensure those mechanisms are appropriate to the needs of the project and the community.

  • Participatory approaches to design the allocation of funding: Ensuring transparency and stakeholder involvement, to the extent possible, in decisions about the project, including the allocation of funding, is an important part of building support for the project.

  • Mechanisms to monitor the project’s negative impacts: Fixing unintended and inadvertent consequences of a project requires that they are identified and rectified as quickly as possible. it is important to conduct regular monitoring throughout the life of the project, as well as consultations with beneficiaries, staff and partners to identify any emerging problems and ensure they are swiftly addressed. Setting up a data collection system can help monitor the context's evolution and emergence of sources of tension and cohesion, and the good implementation of conflict sensitivity measures. 

Further reading:

Contributions to peace and stability

Climate adaptation and mitigation projects can and should go beyond conflict management (‘doing no harm’) to actually being positive for peace. This means looking for ways in which the project can actively look to foster cooperation and communication between different groups that may have a history of tension and conflict, to support existing mechanisms to resolve grievances more effectively, and to encourage natural resource management systems that are inclusive, sustainable and just.  There are a number of ways this can be done:

  • Set clear and measurable goals related to peace: The way to develop a project that is peace positive is to consider what the project can achieve at the outset, to set appropriate SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound) indicators, and to monitor the delivery of those indicators throughout the project. For example, for projects in the Green Climate Fund, we would recomend  to include co-benefit(s) indicator(s) and target(s) on peace in the Theory of Change and outcome mapping of the GCF results areas and co-benefits sections, in line with the GCF Integrated Results Management Framework policy.

  • Support employment and livelihoods for local communities: Projects setting up, for example, solar microgrids or similar renewable energy infrastructure can support livelihood diversification and provide economic opportunities for displaced populations and youth, especially in rural areas. This can involve training and capacity-building programs to develop skills related to energy entrepreneurship. By providing stable economic alternatives and more clean and accessible energy to communities, renewable energy interventions can also reduce the pressure on natural resources and the environment, thus contributing to more secure livelihoods for people and communities.

  • Consider the needs, preferences and concerns of women, youth and marginalised groups Every social grouping is different, with varied needs and differing challenges. It is important to conduct a gender and social inclusion analysis in line with the objectives of the project. During the design and development of the project, ensure that community and indigenous groups are fully engaged. Have a strategic plan for their engagement during the lifecycle of the project/programme of the funding proposal.

  • Find opportunities to promote dialogue and build trust: Climate finance projects can bring different groups together in ways that promote dialogue and forge practical links among communities, so building trust between or among different groups. Using participatory approaches in the design and/ or implementation of the project can help tailor the project and its objectives to the target country.

  • Look for opportunities to strengthen state-society relations or local institutions: Climate finance projects can help to improve relationships between state and local communities, and also support local institutions’ efforts for conflict prevention or resolution. To do so it is important to map during the project planning stage what structures and institutions exist and then work to include them in project consultations and implementation.

  • Identify opportunities for dialogue and collaboration: It is important to foster partnerships with Civil Society Organisations (CSOs) to attempt to resolve such conflicts through peacebuilding actions, for example, by promoting regulatory frameworks and strong rule of law. Using participatory approaches in the development of project design and/or implementation can help tailoring the project and its objectives to the target country.

Exit strategy

A peace-responsive exit strategy can help to ensure that the peace-positive impacts of the project can persist. When drafting the exit strategy, identify and assess the potential impacts the withdrawal or exit of the programme may have on conflict dynamics. Some guiding questions to evaluate the impact of the withdrawal/exit can include:

  • How will you ensure that the positive impacts of the intervention will be sustained beyond the life of your presence in the area?

  • If you are handing activities over to another agency/actor, do they have the capacities to ensure that the programme continues to be implemented in a conflict-sensitive manner? If not, do you have a plan to capacitate them for this transition?

  • How will you ensure that the lessons learned from this programme can be easily accessible and/or communicated to relevant people working in this context or other places facing similar challenges?

Expert advice

There is a wealth of information on conflict-sensitivity and peace responsive investment and programming. Many of the challenges you might face have been faced, and met before, so we would encourage you to seek advice and support from peer entities and specialized local and international peacebuilding organizations on project design and implementation, and share your ideas and lessons learned on what has worked well.

Often, prominent local peacebuilding organizations become apparent through initial engagement with local communities who can recommend civil society partners. In that case, it is important to ensure that the potential partner fulfils due diligence criteria. Due diligence is essential in engaging local civil society actors, particularly in conflict contexts, where organisations from geographic zones different to those where the project is being implemented may not have the same access as local ones and could cause more harm. Examples of considerations for selecting a local civil society partner include:

  • Does the organization have peace or conflict reduction as one of its organizational goals?

  • Has the organization been operational in the geographic area for more than two years?

  • Is the organisation recognized and trusted by different communities in the geographic focus area?

  • Are the staff members representative of different communities in the project area?

  • Are staff members gender balanced?

  • Does the organisation possess the right technical capacities (or technical potential) to assist your project? Does it have experience with similar work?

Some major donor government embassies in the country or region may also be able to help with finding a credible local peacebuilding partner. Major government donors who support peacebuilding organizations would have a focal point who oversees the embassy’s peacebuilding or conflict-related work. In all cases, independent due diligence will remain essential to mitigating risks when selecting a local peacebuilding partner.

Guidance Note for Peace-Informed Programming at the Green Climate Fund

Summary: Energy plays a key role in human activities, driving economies and societies worldwide. Notably, 85 percent of the 800 million people without energy access live in fragile states. Conflict Assessment

Consider conducting a conflict assessment.

Considering the (sometimes complex) interactions between the impacts of climate change and conflict is particularly important in an area vulnerable to climate change and affected by underlying tensions, whether they are rooted in ethnic, economic, linguistic or social differences. The impacts of climate change can worsen these dividing lines, risking the smooth implementation of project activities. In addition, projects can sometimes unintentionally make the situation worse if the project is ‘blind’ to those conflict drivers. But equally, being aware of the conflict drivers enables the project to have a ‘peace-positive’ impact.

A conflict assessment (or a climate-conflict assessment) is an important first step to understand the underlying drivers of conflict in your project area, to anticipate problems, to ensure your project is not making them worse, and to develop ‘peace-positive’ solutions. Using a conflict ‘lens’ is an important way to think through the risks of the project being derailed by, or adding to, conflict. It can be done as a part of the broad socioeconomic risk assessment, or as a separate, additional exercise.

The process of developing your conflict assessment can be as important as the product itself. It is an opportunity to engage with your stakeholders to better understand their needs and concerns. It is also a chance to involve as many of the project team as possible to raise their awareness of the political and social dynamics in the project area. The conflict assessment should be a ‘living document’ – something that you can come back to and use time and again as the project evolves.

The conflict assessment should contain the following elements, depending on the needs of your specific project. First, you can use the assessment already done under the climate rationale on the current and projected climate impacts to assess the coping and adaptation capacity and how the project may affect conflict dynamics in the project area. Second, you can build on the mapping of the social and environmental dynamics around the natural resources affected by the project (e.g. land, water, energy) as part of your compliance to ESS and Indigenous People's policies to forecast how the project might/ will change access, control and use of those resources. Finally, utilize your consultation and stakeholder engagement plans to assess who will be affected by the project, how and to what extent.

The conflict assessment needs to:

  • Involve local stakeholders: This is essential to ensuring that the project properly anticipates potential conflict issues that could derail the project, ensures that the project does no harm by exacerbating underlying causes of tension, and maximises the opportunities to support peace in the project area.
  • Consider the potential intersections between climate change impacts and conflict drivers: Considering the (sometimes complex) interactions between the impacts of climate change and conflict is particularly important in an area vulnerable to climate change and affected by underlying tensions, whether they are rooted in ethnic, economic, linguistic or social differences. The impacts of climate change can worsen these dividing lines, and risk the smooth implementation of project activities. In addition, projects can sometimes unintentionally make the situation worse if the project is ‘blind’ to those conflict drivers. Equally, being aware of the conflict drivers enables the project to have a ‘peace-positive’ impact.
  • Consider the potential constraining factors for the participation, benefit and empowerment of specific groups: Considering how stakeholders can participate in, benefit from and be empowered by the project is a critical part of designing a successful, impactful project. This is particularly important for sometimes marginalised groups that may otherwise be left out of the design or implementation of the project: such as women, youth, older people, minorities, and the disabled. Involving these stakeholders in the production of a conflict assessment (through focus group discussions, consultations, and - where appropriate - in the analysis team itself)  is essential to ensuring that the project includes mechanisms to ensure the full participation of these groups in the project and anticipates potential conflict that could derail the project, ensures that the project does no harm by exacerbating underlying causes of tension, and maximises the opportunities to support peace in the project area.
  • Quantify the conflict and security risks in the project: Your conflict assessment should try to outline the conflict and security risks in the proposed project and understand the extent to which they pose threats to the stakeholders of the project, to project staff and/or the successful achievement of the project’s goals. Where appropriate, your funding proposal should identify external conflict as a potential risk and outline a plan for mitigating conflict and promoting peace in the targeted region/area. While developing the Theory of Change of the funding proposal, you can identify the pitfalls and levers to mitigate security impacts and maximise co-benefits to increase the resilience of the most vulnerable people and communities by defining the components that can deepen the rift between communities, or, on the contrary, bring them together.
